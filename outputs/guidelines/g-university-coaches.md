# An Autistic Student in Your Sport / Society

> **For university sport, gym & society coaches and leaders** — supporting an autistic **student** (roughly ages 18–25).

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means a student's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." University clubs can be a genuine refuge — structure, shared interest, belonging — for a student navigating a chaotic new life. But the social, sensory and initiation demands of club life (initiations, loud socials, last-minute changes) can also push a depleted student into **burnout**. Lean into the structure and the interest, lower the social/sensory load, and your club becomes the steady anchor many autistic students need.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Brilliant and reliable at training; skips the social | The sport fits; the social doesn't — high-channel load, not aloofness |
| Burns out mid-term; stops coming | Cumulative university load tipping into **burnout** |
| Intense focus on technique, kit or a single role | **Monotropic strength** — give them that role |
| Won't do initiations, loud socials or group chats | Sensory/social overload — offer low-pressure alternatives |
| Takes instructions literally; prefers written plans | Literal, single-channel communication |

## Try this {.do}
- **Make training predictable** — consistent time, place, plan; share the session plan in advance in writing.
- **Show, then say; one focus at a time**; use video or a whiteboard for tactics.
- **Offer low-sensory, low-pressure social options** alongside (not instead of) the sport; never make initiations mandatory.
- **Use the interest and strengths** — a defined role (kit, stats, setup) gives certainty and belonging.
- **Check in literally and privately** if a reliable regular stops coming; burnout needs reduced load, not a pep talk.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Surprise socials, initiations, loud buses or last-minute fixture changes with no notice.
- Equating club commitment with attending every loud social event.
- Reading a withdrawal or a literal question as disengagement.

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout** (exhaustion, loss of skills, reduced tolerance) and signpost the **student wellbeing/disability service**. Keep membership flexible; a reduced or social-only role mid-term can keep a struggling student connected while they recover.

## If the student is a woman {.gender}
> Women and marginalised-gender students mask intensely and are often late-identified; a "sociable high-achiever" may be privately exhausted. Take reduced tolerance and withdrawal seriously.
