# An Autistic Adult in Your Training / Course

> **For trainers, vocational coaches, mentors & CPD instructors** — teaching an autistic **adult learner**.

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means an adult learner's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." In the right format, that focus is a real asset — depth, retention and attention to detail. But most training assumes polytropic habits: rapid task-switching, group exercises, "ice-breakers," verbal-only instructions and last-minute changes — all of which tax a monotropic learner. Small adjustments (clarity, predictability, written materials, fewer forced switches) make your training accessible to everyone and let an autistic learner's strengths show.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Engrossed in one module; "checks out" elsewhere | Deep, single-track focus — channel it, don't fight it |
| Won't join pair-work or "ice-breakers" | High social load, not disengagement; learns better solo or in writing |
| Asks for detail, rules, or "why" repeatedly | Literal, single-channel processing — a sign of engagement |
| Falls behind after an overload of group days | Probable overwhelm/burnout, not lack of effort |
| Communicates better by email/chat than aloud | Single-channel communication; writing reduces the load |

## Try this {.do}
- **Put materials and instructions in writing**, in advance — slides, handouts, schedules and criteria; avoid "listen and remember" only.
- **Predictability over surprise** — a clear agenda, advance notice of changes, a quiet break-out space.
- **Offer real alternatives** to forced group work and participation — solo tasks, written reflection, a defined role in a group.
- **One instruction at a time, with reasons**; allow processing time and written follow-up.
- **Play to the interest and strength** where you can; depth is where autistic learners often shine.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Last-minute changes, surprise "share with the group" demands, or cold-calling.
- Equating eye contact or verbal fluency with engagement or competence.
- Piling on simultaneous channels (talk + read + discuss + decide) without structure.

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for signs of **overwhelm or burnout** (exhaustion, withdrawal, dropping skills) and check in privately and literally. Signpost workplace mentoring, **Access to Work / disability services**, and reasonable adjustments; an autistic adult is the expert on what helps — ask them.

## If the learner is a woman {.gender}
> Women are over-represented among late-diagnosed adults and mask heavily, so exhaustion and difference may be hidden behind a competent exterior. Don't let a polished surface override signs of overload.
