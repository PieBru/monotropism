# Your Autistic Friend / Neighbour (an Older Adult)

> **For friends, neighbours & community peers** — being a good friend to an **older autistic adult** (roughly 60+).

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means an older friend's attention has always locked into a few deep, intense "tunnels." Many were never identified and may simply be "the quiet, routine-loving one" — and **social isolation** is a real, serious risk in this group. A steady, predictable, low-pressure friendship — connection through a shared interest, no demands to "perform" warmth the typical way, and patience with a different style — can be one of the most protective things in their life.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Same routine, same topic, same seat | Predictability and a lifelong interest — **anchors**, not rigidity |
| Quiet; avoids groups, loud places, surprises | Managing a lifelong sensory/social load |
| Slow to answer; asks you to repeat | Processing time + possible hearing loss — not disinterest |
| Takes things literally; misses banter | Literal communication — say what you mean |
| Declines or cancels more than other friends | A finite budget, spent carefully — not about you |

## Try this {.do}
- **Connect through the interest** — it's where they're most themselves; let them share expertise.
- **Keep it predictable and low-pressure** — same time, same plan, no surprises.
- **Say what you mean**; allow processing time and repeats.
- **Offer low-key, quieter ways to meet** — a walk, a coffee, side-by-side over a crowd.
- **Leave the door open** — "no need to reply"; consistency beats intensity.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Loud surprises, group pressure, or "come on, live a little."
- Reading quiet, a different style or a slow reply as disinterest.
- Assuming slowness or a change means decline — flag it kindly if worried.

## When to get more support {.support}
> **Social isolation** is a major risk for older autistic adults — your friendship is genuinely protective. If you notice a real change in skills, mood or engagement, share it kindly and, if appropriate, with family — distinguishing lifelong difference from decline needs careful assessment, not assumption.

## If your friend is a woman {.gender}
> Older autistic women are especially under-identified, having masked for a lifetime; "quiet and anxious" may be unrecognised autism.
