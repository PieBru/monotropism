# An Autistic Child in Your Childcare / Nursery

> **For childminders, nursery staff & early-years carers** — caring for an autistic **pre-school child** (roughly ages 2–5).

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means a young child's attention is pulled into a few deep, intense "tunnels" at a time. Childcare is busy, noisy and full of transitions — genuinely hard for a monotropic child, *even one who loves to play*. You're often the person who keeps the child's day predictable and safe, and sometimes the first to spot early signs. Consistent staff, a calm routine, lower sensory load and an interest-led approach let a keen, settled child emerge — and a sudden change in behaviour usually means unmet need, not naughtiness.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Meltdown or goes floppy/silent at drop-off or tidy-up | Involuntary response to transition or overload — not naughtiness |
| Won't eat, drink, or use the toilet when absorbed | Things outside the tunnel **don't register**; gentle specific reminders help |
| Distressed by noise, lights, certain textures | Sensory differences — real discomfort, not fussiness |
| Lines up, sorts, repeats; plays "differently" | **Monotropic play** — valid and meaningful |
| Upset by a change of staff, room or routine | Predictability and people are **safety anchors** |

## Try this {.do}
- **Keep staff, room and sequence consistent**; warn before every change with a visual timer and a bridge object.
- **Lower the sensory load** — a quiet, low-light calm corner; ear-defenders; reduce clutter and glare.
- **Join their interest first**, then extend; honour lining-up and sorting as real play.
- **Gentle, specific reminders** for food, drink and toilet ("toilet break now?") — the need may have dropped out of awareness.
- **Have a calm meltdown/shutdown plan**: reduce input, ensure safety, don't reason or restrain, give time.
- **Share a two-way note with parents** — consistency across home and childcare prevents many hard days.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Surprise changes, forced eye contact, or holding the child down.
- Stopping stimming or treating lining-up/sorting as "wrong" play.
- Reading a calm few minutes as "they're fine" before a sudden change.

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **overwhelm/"regression"** — a loss of skills usually means *reduce demands and restore rest and interests*, not push harder. Investigate **pain or illness first** for any new distress (teething, ear infection, constipation). Flag early signs supportively with parents; early help adapts the environment, not the child.

## If the child is a girl {.gender}
> Girls camouflage early and are missed more often; a quiet, compliant surface can hide real overload. Take parental concern seriously.
