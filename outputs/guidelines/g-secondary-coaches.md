# An Autistic Teenager in Your Coaching / Activity

> **For sports coaches, PE teachers & activity leaders** — supporting an autistic **teenager** (roughly ages 12–18).

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means a teen's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." Sport can be a lifeline at this age — a place of routine, mastery, friendship and recovery from the social drain of school — *if* the environment fits. This is the **peak age for anxiety, depression and burnout**, and many autistic teens mask heavily all day and arrive at training already depleted. Predictability, sensory kindness, clear roles and a coach who "gets it" can make your club the safest hour of their week.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Exhausted, "can't be bothered," skips sessions | Probable **burnout** or depletion — not lack of commitment |
| Meltdown or shutdown at the whistle or after a loss | Involuntary response to overload; shame makes it worse |
| Intense focus on technique, stats or one event | **Monotropic strength** — a route to mastery and identity |
| Won't join the team huddle or social after | High social load, not rudeness — give a defined role instead |
| Takes feedback literally; needs a moment to answer | Literal, single-channel communication + processing time |

## Try this {.do}
- **Predictable structure and routine**; warn before any change to drills, teams or fixtures.
- **Show, then say; one instruction at a time**; use video/whiteboard for tactics.
- **Manage sensory load** — quieter spaces, ear-defenders, warning before whistles/buzzers; offer individual options.
- **Give a clear role** and use the interest (the stat-lover keeps the book; the routine-lover leads the warm-up).
- **Focus on mastery and relationship** — a teen who trusts you will push through more; celebrate effort, not just results.
- **Have an overload plan** — an agreed quiet space and a signal to step out, no questions asked.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Surprise changes, public criticism, or "tough love" that shames a shutdown.
- Forcing eye contact, team socials, or huddles with no opt-out.
- Equating a masked session or a withdrawal with "not trying."

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout** — exhaustion, loss of skills, reduced tolerance — and take it seriously; rest and reduced load are recovery, not weakness. Liaise with parents if you see crisis signs, and keep your club a low-demand, accepting space. High-quality friendship through sport is powerfully protective.

## If the teen is a girl or marginalised gender {.gender}
> Camouflaging peaks here; a "quiet, capable, sociable-on-the-surface" teen may be running on empty. Watch for private collapse and check in literally and privately.
