# Your Older Autistic Relative

> **For adult children, spouses & family** — supporting an older autistic adult (roughly ages 60+), diagnosed or not.

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means your older relative's attention has always locked into a few deep, intense "tunnels." This is the **least-researched part of the autistic lifespan**, and the news is sobering: older autistic adults have **higher rates of most health conditions, low quality of life and marked social isolation**, and many were never identified — the "lost generation." A lifetime of "being a bit eccentric," strong routines, social exhaustion and burnout may, in hindsight, be autism. Lifelong interests and routines are anchors of wellbeing; your understanding matters enormously.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Avoids care, misses appointments, "doesn't like coming in" | Lifelong negative experiences + sensory/communication barriers — not non-compliance |
| Decline in skills, exhaustion, withdrawal | Possibly **autistic burnout** accumulated over decades — often misread as "ageing" |
| Slow, unusual responses; seems confused | Often processing time + lifelong autistic difference — **not necessarily dementia** |
| Strong routines, objects, interests; resists change | These are **protective anchors** for identity and cognition |
| Doesn't report pain or symptoms | **Interoception** differences; "not complaining" ≠ "not suffering" |

## Try this {.do}
- **Believe and reframe** — a lifetime of difference finally making sense can be **enormously validating**; let them lead on whether a formal label matters to them.
- **Protect lifelong routines, interests and objects** — removing them "for safety" can cause real harm.
- **Communicate clearly, literally and slowly**, in writing where it helps; allow ample processing time.
- **Lower sensory load** and account for hearing/vision changes that compound baseline differences.
- **Plan ahead, early and gently** — housing, finances, legal arrangements, benefits, social networks and end-of-life wishes — with your relative leading.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Writing off lifelong differences as "just ageing," or burnout as depression.
- Disrupting routines or removing meaningful objects in hospital or care.
- Assuming cognitive incapacity from a slow or atypical response — use supported decision-making.

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout** (often decades in the making) and treat it as distinct from depression. The hardest question — **dementia, or autistic difference?** — is genuinely hard and under-researched: don't assume; get an **autism-informed** assessment (standard screens can misread autistic traits). Address **social isolation** (a major risk) and be transparent about the thin evidence in old age.

## If your relative is a woman {.gender}
> Older autistic women are an especially under-identified group, having masked for a lifetime. A history of "anxiety," misdiagnoses or burnout alongside lifelong social/sensory difference warrants serious consideration of autism.
