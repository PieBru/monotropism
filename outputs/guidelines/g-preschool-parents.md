# Your Autistic Pre-School Child

> **For parents & family carers** — understanding and supporting an autistic **pre-school child** (roughly ages 2–5).

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means your child's attention is pulled into a few deep, intense "tunnels" at a time. Inside a tunnel, focus is vivid and rich; outside it, things barely register — sometimes including hunger, pain, or someone speaking. This single difference explains the intense interests, the sensory reactions, the love of routine, and the meltdowns at changeover. Your child isn't "behind" or "broken" — their mind waters a smaller patch of ground, *deeply*. Your greatest power is an environment that works *with* that attention.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Deeply absorbed in one object or topic; lines things up; plays "differently" | **Monotropic play** — valid and meaningful; often the child's route to calm and learning |
| Doesn't respond to name; "ignores" hunger, thirst, the toilet | When absorbed, things outside the tunnel **genuinely don't register** |
| Sudden crying, yelling, going floppy or silent | An involuntary **meltdown** or **shutdown** from lights, noise or the wait — *not* naughtiness |
| Strong reactions to sounds, lights, textures, food — or seems to feel nothing | Sensory differences: a stimulus *in* the tunnel is felt intensely; one *out* may not register |
| Slow to respond; repeats your words back | Processing time + single-channel, literal communication |

## Try this {.do}
- **Join their interest before you lead.** Enter their tunnel and play *their* way first — connection and early learning (language, counting, turn-taking) all build from there.
- **Make the day predictable** — steady routines for waking, meals, play and bedtime; warn before every change ("first shoes, then park") and use photos or a simple visual timetable.
- **Lower the sensory load** — a quiet, low-light corner; ear-defenders for loud places; seamless clothes with the tags cut out.
- **Use "declarative" language** — observations and reflections ("I see your coat by the door") instead of constant commands and questions; it lowers the pressure on a nervous system that bristles at demands.
- **Have a meltdown/shutdown plan**: stay calm, lower the load (quiet, dim, fewer people), don't reason or judge in the moment, give time, keep everyone safe.
- **Follow your instincts and refer early** if you notice slowed/"sticky" attention, intense interests, sensory differences or atypical play — early help adapts the environment, not the child.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Forcing eye contact, holding them down, or springing a change by surprise.
- Treating lining-up, sorting or sensory play as "wrong" play to be corrected.
- Reading a calm few minutes out and about as "they're fine" — they may pay for it with a shutdown afterwards.

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout / "regression"** — a sudden loss of skills usually means *overwhelm*, and the response is to **reduce demands and restore rest and interests**, not push harder. Ask your health visitor or GP about an autism assessment — you don't need to be "deserving" to be referred. Seek **autism-led parent support** (it tends to be the most accurate and affirming), and care for siblings and your own wellbeing too.

## If your child is a girl {.gender}
> Girls are often better at early **camouflaging** and are missed more often. Take your own concern seriously even when the presentation looks "sociable" or "subtle" — masked effort is real effort.
