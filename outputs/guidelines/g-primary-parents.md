# Your Autistic Primary-School Child

> **For parents & family carers** — understanding and supporting an autistic **primary-school child** (roughly ages 6–11).

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means your child's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." That tunnel is their engine — for joy, calm, skill and identity. The biggest gift you can give is an environment that works *with* their attention, not against it: predictable routines, protected interests, lower sensory load, and time to recover from the effort of holding it together at school.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Intense focus on one topic, sometimes for years | A **passion** that brings joy, calm and learning — not an "obsession" to limit |
| "Fine at school," meltdown or shutdown the moment they're home | Home is the **safe place** where the day's mask finally drops — a sign of trust and exhaustion |
| Won't hear you; "ignores" hunger, thirst, the toilet | When absorbed, things outside the tunnel **genuinely don't register** |
| Slow to start, stop or switch; meltdowns at transitions | **Autistic inertia** — changing track costs real energy |
| Strong reactions to sounds, lights, clothes, food | Sensory differences: a stimulus *in* the tunnel is felt intensely; one *out* may not register |

## Try this {.do}
- **Protect and use the interest.** Build maths, reading and connection *through* it; never "wean" a healthy passion. Join it sometimes — sitting alongside with genuine curiosity is powerful.
- **Make the day predictable** — steady routines for mornings, after school, meals and bedtime — and give warnings before every transition ("10 minutes… 2 minutes…").
- **Lower the sensory load** at home: a quiet corner, dimmable light, headphones for noisy places, comfortable/seamless clothes.
- **Reduce demands on hard days.** If they held it together at school, expect a crash afterwards — build in calm, interest-led recovery time.
- **Have a meltdown/shutdown plan**: stay calm, lower the load (quiet, dim, fewer people), don't reason or judge in the moment, give time, keep everyone safe.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Treating the interest as a problem to manage, or the meltdown as naughtiness.
- Forcing eye contact, stillness or extra demands when they're already depleted.
- Piling on after-school activities and social plans — rest is a need, not laziness.

## When to get more support {.support}
> A sudden drop in skills usually signals **overwhelm/burnout**, not lost ability — respond by reducing demands and restoring rest and interests. Advocate at school (SENCO/SEND, a quiet space, interest-led learning). Watch for **anxiety and bullying**. Seek autism-led family support and a positively-framed understanding of autism for siblings.

## If your child is a girl {.gender}
> Girls camouflage heavily and are often missed — the quiet, "anxious perfectionist" may be an exhausted autistic child. Trust your instincts and push for assessment if school sees only a compliant surface.
