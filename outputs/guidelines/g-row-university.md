# Everyone Around an Autistic University Student

> **The cumulative guide for the whole team around an autistic student** (ages ~18–25) — family, lecturers & university staff, coaches/society leaders, doctors & student-health nurses, disability-support workers, employers (internships/jobs) and peers. *An extended reference sheet; pair it with the single-counterpart quick guides.*

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means a student's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." University is a *massive* transition — new autonomy, less structure, open-plan everything, group work and social pressure — and many autistic students who thrived at school **hit a wall** (burnout, anxiety or crisis) in year one or two. Their focused interests are a genuine academic strength; the system around them is usually the problem. Whatever your role, the levers are the same: protect focus, add predictability, communicate in writing and literally, and treat burnout as the real, distinct thing it is. A coordinated, accepting team is often the difference between a student flourishing and one who quietly leaves.

## What helps across every setting {.do}
- **Protect focus and the special interest** — structure study, work and support around fewer, deeper "tunnels, not tasks"; never limit a healthy passion.
- **Add predictability** — clear plans, advance notice of changes, consistent people; put essentials in writing.
- **Communicate in writing and literally**, one thing at a time; allow processing time; offer asynchronous options.
- **Lower sensory and social load** — quieter spaces, fewer forced group/social demands, flexible participation.
- **Treat burnout as real** — exhaustion, loss of skills and reduced tolerance need *reduced load and unmasking*, not "push through"; a temporary reduction in load or interruption of studies is a legitimate recovery step.
- **Know the signposts and go *with* them** — disability service, wellbeing, mentoring, autistic student communities, crisis lines.

## What to avoid across every setting {.dont}
- Defaulting to "typical student stress" and missing burnout or a first identification.
- Surprise changes, mandatory group work/cold-calling with no alternative, or "push through" advice that increases masking.
- Equating silence, a flat affect or self-neglect with "choosing not to engage."
- Making decisions *for* them — support autonomy and choice.

## Quick notes for each person around the student

### Family {.stage}
Believe them. Help with the practicals if asked (disability service, accommodations, appointments, finances). Normalise reduced load and protect their downtime; communicate in writing, on their terms. Take private collapse and withdrawal seriously.

### Lecturers & university staff
Put slides, instructions, deadlines and criteria in writing and in advance; offer flexible participation and alternative assessment; signpost the disability service early. A reduced load in a crisis is a recovery step, not failure.

### Coaches & society leaders
Make training predictable and share the plan in advance; offer low-sensory, low-pressure social options and never make initiations mandatory. Keep membership flexible; a reduced role mid-term keeps a struggling student connected.

### Doctors & student-health nurses
Recognise **autistic burnout** and screen for suicidality directly. Offer written/online appointments and a supporter; treat self-neglect and withdrawal as unmet need. Signpost wellbeing, mentoring and crisis support.

### Disability-support workers & mentors
Be the predictable relationship; communicate in writing; protect focus and break tasks into steps. Normalise extensions/deferrals; know the signposts and go with them. Check in literally when they go quiet.

### Employers (internships / part-time jobs)
Be explicit about expectations and unwritten rules; offer written instructions and protected focus; give literal, kind feedback. A placement alongside study is a lot — flexibility prevents burnout.

### Peers & flatmates
Agree flat norms early and in writing; default to text; protect quiet time. Connect through the interest and check in literally. If they talk about hopelessness, don't keep it to yourself.

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout** (exhaustion, loss of skills, reduced tolerance) and **suicidality** — screen and ask directly. Co-occurring **ADHD, anxiety, sleep and GI conditions** deserve autism-informed care. A temporary reduction in academic load or interruption of studies is an evidence-aligned recovery step, not failure.

## If the student is a woman {.gender}
> Women and marginalised-gender students are over-represented among late-identified students and mask intensely. A "high-achieving, sociable-on-the-surface" presentation with private collapse is a key pattern — take the gap between public performance and private exhaustion seriously across every setting.
