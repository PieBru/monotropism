# An Autistic Student in Your Lecture / Seminar

> **For lecturers, tutors & university teaching staff** — supporting an autistic **university / college student** (roughly ages 18–25).

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means a student's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." University is a *massive* transition, and many autistic students who thrived at school **hit a wall** in year one or two — burnout, anxiety, missed deadlines or crisis. Their focused interests are a genuine academic strength (deep expertise, sustained concentration, original thinking); the system around them is usually the problem. Small, predictable adjustments to how you teach and assess can be the difference between a student flourishing and one who quietly disappears.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Brilliant on their interest; "absent" elsewhere | Deep, single-track focus — a strength to channel, not a gap to penalise |
| Stops attending, misses deadlines | **Autistic burnout**, inertia or executive overload — not laziness |
| Won't speak in seminars; avoids group work | High-channel social load; prefers to process and contribute in writing |
| First identified only now, in crisis | University is a common late-identification point |
| Takes instructions literally; asks for detail | Literal, single-channel communication — engagement, not awkwardness |

## Try this {.do}
- **Put the essentials in writing** — slides, instructions, deadlines and criteria in advance; record lectures; avoid "read the room" expectations.
- **Offer flexibility in participation** — written contributions, alternative assessment, a choice of topics that allows an interest-led route.
- **Protect predictability** — clear structure, advance notice of changes, asynchronous options where possible.
- **Signpost the disability service early** and work with their agreed adjustments (quiet exam space, mentor, note-taker).
- **Reduce, don't escalate, in a crisis** — a temporary extension or reduced load is a legitimate recovery step.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Defaulting to "typical student stress" and missing burnout or a first identification.
- Mandatory group work, cold-calling or participation grades with no alternative.
- Penalising literal questions or a different communication style as "disengagement."

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout** (exhaustion, loss of skills, reduced tolerance to stimulus) and signs of crisis — refer to the **disability/wellbeing service**, mentoring and autistic student communities. A temporary reduction in load or interruption of studies is an evidence-aligned recovery step, not failure.

## If the student is a woman {.gender}
> Women and marginalised-gender students are over-represented among late-identified students and mask intensely. A "high-achieving, sociable-on-the-surface" student who privately collapses is a key pattern — take the gap between public performance and private exhaustion seriously.
