# An Autistic Colleague at Work

> **For colleagues, managers & employers** — working alongside an autistic **adult**.

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means a colleague's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." In the right environment that's a genuine asset — sustained concentration, reliability, depth, pattern-detection and honesty. In the wrong one (open-plan noise, constant interruption, office politics, vague expectations) it drives masking and **autistic burnout**. Most accommodations that help an autistic colleague — clarity, predictability, fewer interruptions, written communication — make the workplace better for everyone. You don't need to be an expert; curiosity and small adjustments go a long way.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Deep, focused work; dislikes meetings & chatter | **Monotropic focus** — a strength; protect it |
| Wants emails over calls; takes things literally | Single-channel, literal communication — not awkwardness |
| Exhausted, withdrawn, or "off" after high-social days | Heavy **masking**; the cost is paid later |
| Misses unwritten rules or office politics | Not "playing the game" — genuinely doesn't see those channels |
| Loyal, reliable, bluntly honest | Often the colleague who spots the problem no one else named |

## Try this {.do}
- **Be clear and explicit** — written briefs, defined deadlines, named expectations; say the "unwritten rules."
- **Protect focus** — fewer interruptions, fewer meetings, asynchronous options, a quieter spot.
- **Give literal, kind feedback** — say what you mean; assume good intent in blunt exchanges.
- **Make social optional** — don't make team belonging depend on the pub or loud events.
- **Ask what helps** — the person is the expert; reasonable adjustments (Equality Act / ADA) are a right.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Vague briefs, last-minute changes, or surprise "quick chats."
- Equating eye contact, small talk or "visibility" with commitment or competence.
- Reading a burnout withdrawal as "not a team player."

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout** (chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, reduced tolerance) — it's linked to suicidality and is distinct from ordinary work stress. Reasonable adjustments, reduced load or time off are recovery, not weakness. Signpost occupational health, employee assistance and autistic-led workplace resources.

## If your colleague is a woman {.gender}
> Late-diagnosed women mask most heavily and may look "high-performing" while privately exhausted; take overload and a request for adjustments seriously, regardless of how together they seem.
