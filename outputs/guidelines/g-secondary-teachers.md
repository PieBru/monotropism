# An Autistic Student in Your Secondary-School Class

> **For secondary-school teachers, form tutors & heads of year** — supporting an autistic **teenager** (roughly ages 12–18).

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means a student's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." Secondary school multiplies the load: changing rooms every lesson, new peer hierarchies, puberty, exams, and corridors and dining halls that are "hell for many monotropic kids — even though a lot of us love learning." This is the **peak age for anxiety, depression and burnout**, and many autistic teens (especially girls and marginalised genders) are **first identified only in crisis**. Resistance at changeover is a cognitive-cost problem, not defiance; their intense interests are a route to engagement and identity, not a distraction.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Deep interest; resists other work | A **passion** and the best bridge to the curriculum — not a distraction |
| "Defiant" at transitions; school refusal | **Autistic inertia** + overwhelm — changing track costs real energy |
| Meltdown or withdrawal in corridors, at break or lunch | Unstructured, noisy time is often the **hardest** part of the day |
| "Fine" in class, then a crisis | Heavy **masking**; the cost is paid later |
| Slow to answer; takes things literally; "rude" | Single-channel, literal communication + processing time |

## Try this {.do}
- **Use the special interest as a bridge** to the subject — projects, reading, research and assessment *through* it.
- **Predictability lowers the load**: visible timetable, seating plan, warnings before changes, a reliable calm space and a named adult.
- **Give instructions in writing**, one thing at a time, with reasons; allow processing time.
- **Protect unstructured time** — a lunch club, library job or quiet space is often the difference between coping and crisis.
- **Take their inner life seriously** — ask directly, literally, about workload, anxiety and friendships; believe them.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Using the interest only as a reward to withhold, or insisting on eye contact/stillness.
- Piling on verbal instructions, surprise changes, or forced group work without preparation.
- Reading a masked, compliant lesson as "they're fine," or a shutdown as refusal.

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout / "regression"** — a sudden drop in skills usually means *overwhelm*; respond by **reducing demands and restoring rest and interests**, not pushing harder. Liaise with **parents, the SENCO/SEND lead** and any autism outreach team. **Screen for anxiety, depression and suicidality** and flag bullying early — high-quality friendship is powerfully protective.

## If the student is a girl or marginalised gender {.gender}
> Camouflaging peaks here; the quiet, "anxious perfectionist" is often a missed autistic student. Take subtle differences and parental concern seriously, and watch for private collapse behind a competent surface.
