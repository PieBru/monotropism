# Everyone Around an Autistic Working Adult

> **The cumulative guide for the whole team around an autistic adult in employment** — family & partners, trainers & mentors, coaches/gym leaders, doctors & occupational-health nurses, job coaches & support workers, colleagues & employers, and friends. *An extended reference sheet; pair it with the single-counterpart quick guides.*

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means an adult's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." In the right job that focus is a genuine superpower — depth, reliability, pattern-detection, honesty. In the wrong environment (open-plan noise, constant interruption, office politics, vague expectations) it drives masking and **autistic burnout**, which is distinct from ordinary occupational stress and depression. Many working autistic adults — especially women — were identified only in adulthood. Whatever your role, the levers are the same: protect focus, be clear and predictable, communicate in writing and literally, and take burnout as the serious, distinct thing it is.

## What helps across every setting {.do}
- **Protect focus and the special interest** — structure work around fewer, deeper "tunnels, not tasks"; minimise interruptions and meetings; allow flow time.
- **Be clear and explicit** — written briefs, defined deadlines, named expectations; say the "unwritten rules."
- **Communicate in writing and literally**; allow asynchronous options; give processing time; don't read a pause as resistance.
- **Lower sensory and social load** — quieter spaces, optional social, fewer forced group demands.
- **Take burnout seriously** — chronic exhaustion, loss of skills and reduced tolerance need *reduced load and unmasking*, not "push through"; reasonable adjustments, time off or a role change are recovery, not weakness.
- **Centre their expertise** — the person is the expert on what helps; reasonable adjustments (Equality Act / ADA) are a right.

## What to avoid across every setting {.dont}
- Vague briefs, last-minute changes, surprise "quick chats," or "you should just know" expectations.
- Equating eye contact, small talk or "visibility" with commitment or competence.
- Treating burnout as depression or laziness; pushing the person to mask more.
- Equating a masked, competent hour with being "fine," or "not complaining" with "not suffering."

## Quick notes for each person around the worker

### Family, partners & friends {.stage}
Believe and validate a late identification; back accommodations and rights. Take burnout seriously — reduced demands and unmasking beat "push through." Help with practicals if asked; respect autonomy, including a role change or reduced hours.

### Trainers & mentors
Put materials and instructions in writing, in advance; offer alternatives to forced group work; one instruction at a time with reasons. Play to depth and interest; check in literally on load.

### Coaches, gym & club leaders
Offer written plans and predictable structure; one cue at a time; respect headphones, quiet and routine as regulation. Be alert to burnout; a flexible, low-demand membership helps through hard patches.

### Doctors & occupational-health nurses
Offer the AASPIRE *AHAT* and written-first options; recognise **autistic burnout** (distinct from depression); broker reasonable adjustments. Start medications low and titrate slowly. Assess for suicidality and co-occurring ADHD, anxiety, sleep and GI conditions.

### Job coaches & support workers
Help articulate reasonable adjustments; structure the role around "tunnels, not tasks"; be the predictable, safe check-in. Take burnout seriously and centre their expertise.

### Colleagues & employers
Keep social optional and low-key; protect focus; give literal, kind feedback. Make reasonable adjustments routine. Watch for burnout and signpost OH/employee assistance.

### Friends & peers
Keep friendship low-key and optional; say what you mean; connect through shared interest. If they seem to be burning out or talk about hopelessness, signpost help and don't sit on it alone.

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout** (chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, reduced tolerance) — it's linked to **suicidality** and is distinct from ordinary work stress and depression. Address co-occurring **ADHD (AuDHD), anxiety, depression, sleep and GI conditions** with autism-informed clinicians. Reasonable adjustments, reduced hours or time off are recovery, not weakness.

## If the person is a woman {.gender}
> Women are over-represented among the "lost generation" of late-diagnosed adults and mask most heavily. Take a lifelong pattern of social/sensory difference and private exhaustion seriously across every setting, even with a "high-performing" exterior.
