# An Older Autistic Adult You Support at Home

> **For home-care workers, personal assistants & community support staff** — supporting an **older autistic adult** (roughly 60+).

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means an older adult's attention has always locked into a few deep, intense "tunnels." Many older autistic adults were never identified and carry a lifetime of masking and unmet need. Your visits may be the most predictable, accepting relationship in their week. Protect lifelong routines, interests and objects; lower the sensory load; communicate literally; and watch for the burnout and pain that "not complaining" can hide.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Same routine, objects, interests — every visit | Predictability and flow are **regulation and identity** |
| Avoids care, cancels, "doesn't like you coming" | Lifelong negative experiences + sensory/communication barriers |
| Slow, unusual responses; seems confused | Often processing time + autistic difference — **not necessarily dementia** |
| Doesn't report pain or symptoms | **Interoception** differences; "not complaining" ≠ "not suffering" |
| Withdrawn, exhausted | Possibly decades of accumulated **burnout** |

## Try this {.do}
- **Be the predictable, accepting presence** — same person, same time, same plan; warn before any change.
- **Protect lifelong routines, interests and objects** — removing them "for safety" can cause real harm.
- **Communicate clearly, literally and slowly**, in writing where it helps; allow ample processing time.
- **Lower the sensory load** and account for hearing/vision changes compounding lifelong differences.
- **Ask proactively about pain and symptoms** — don't wait for them to mention it.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Disrupting routines or removing familiar objects.
- Assuming slowness or a different style means cognitive decline — assess, don't assume.
- Equating quiet or a "settled" presentation with being "well."

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout** (often decades in the making) and **social isolation** (a major risk). The **dementia-or-difference?** question is hard and under-researched — flag changes supportively and seek autism-informed assessment rather than assuming. Report any new distress as a possible medical red flag.

## If the person is a woman {.gender}
> Older autistic women are an especially under-identified group, having masked for a lifetime; a history of "anxiety" or burnout alongside lifelong difference warrants serious consideration of autism.
