# An Autistic Parent Who Is Your Colleague

> **For colleagues, managers & employers** — working with an autistic **adult who is also a parent**.

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means a colleague's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." Add parenting — relentless, high-channel and unpredictable — and the burnout risk climbs sharply. Autistic parents are often excellent, deeply focused employees *when* the role fits, but they carry a load most colleagues can't see, and they'll under-report it. Flexibility, clarity and predictable adjustments let a talented colleague keep contributing without running themselves into the ground.

## What you may see — and what's really going on

| You may see… | What's often happening |
|---|---|
| Brilliant when focused; depleted after high-social days | Deep focus plus a double load (work + parenting) |
| Wants fixed hours, remote work, written updates | Predictability and single-channel communication lower the load |
| Goes quiet or "off" after a hard family stretch | Likely overload/burnout, not disengagement |
| Skips the social, the after-work drinks | Managing a finite social/sensory budget — not unfriendly |
| Direct, literal, task-focused | A strength — clarity and reliability — not coldness |

## Try this {.do}
- **Offer real flexibility** — predictable hours, hybrid/remote options, clear advance notice of changes.
- **Communicate in writing** and keep meetings focused; protect their focus time.
- **Take leave and adjustments at face value** — don't make them justify caring needs.
- **Give literal, kind feedback**; check in on load, not just output.
- **Normalise boundaries** — a colleague who protects recovery time is more reliable, not less committed.

## Avoid this {.dont}
- Guilt-tripping over flexible hours, or equating presence with commitment.
- Surprise changes, mandatory socials, or "just a quick favour" pile-ups.
- Reading a depleted, blunt period as "attitude."

## When to get more support {.support}
> Watch for **autistic burnout** (exhaustion, loss of skills, reduced tolerance), which can overlap with perinatal mental-illness. Reasonable adjustments, reduced hours or leave are recovery, not weakness. Signpost occupational health, employee assistance and autism-led support.

## If your colleague is a mother {.gender}
> Autistic mothers are frequently misdiagnosed and mask intensely, including at work; a "coping" surface can hide serious overload. Take requests for flexibility and adjustments seriously.
