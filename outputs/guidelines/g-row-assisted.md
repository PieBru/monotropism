# Everyone Around an Autistic Person in High-Support Care

> **The cumulative guide for the whole team around an autistic person in assisted / residential / high-support care** (any age) — family & relatives, doctors & care-home clinicians, support workers & PAs, and nursing/residential-care staff. *An extended reference sheet; pair it with the single-counterpart quick guides.*

## The one idea {.lead}
Autism, through **monotropism**, means a person's attention locks into a few deep, intense "tunnels." For a high-support autistic person, care environments are often the **hardest of all**: sensory-hostile, routine-disrupting, full of unpredictable touch and demand. Many communicate without speech (AAC, behaviour, a trusted other) and may not register or report pain until severe. The single shared principle for the whole team is radical and simple: **read all behaviour as communication about unmet need** — pain, fear, overload or a lost routine — and protect predictability, objects, interests and agency. Dignity and consent hinge on working *with* the person, never assuming incapacity.

## What helps across every setting {.do}
- **Investigate pain and physical causes first** for any new distress — constipation, dental pain, infection, ill-fitting equipment are common, hidden culprits.
- **Build care around predictability** — consistent staff, same sequence, visual schedules, advance warning of every change.
- **Lower sensory load** — quiet room, dim light, reduce beeps and strong smells; preserve the person's own objects, interests and routines.
- **Use supported decision-making** — explain in an accessible, literal form; allow processing time; consult the trusted supporter and any advance/communication profile.
- **Have a calm, written meltdown/shutdown plan**: reduce stimulation, ensure safety, give time and space — do not restrain or punish.
- **Document and share an individualised sensory, communication and reasonable-adjustments profile** across the whole team.
- **Keep the team coordinated and consistent** — one shared profile and a single set of agreed approaches across family, clinicians, support workers and nursing prevents the distress that staff changes and crossed wires cause.
- **Plan every transition and admission in advance** — a written "health passport", photos of the new setting and a known contact turn the hardest moments (hospital, move, staff change) into manageable ones.
- **Keep communication aids and AAC available and working** — for many, AAC *is* their voice; a missing or dead device is an emergency, not an inconvenience.

## What to avoid across every setting {.dont}
- Labelling distress "behavioural" before excluding pain and overload.
- Using restraint or sedation as a first response to meltdown/shutdown.
- Removing routines, objects or interests "for their own good."
- Assuming lack of capacity from atypical or non-verbal communication.
- Letting communication aids, objects or routines get "lost" at handovers or admissions — continuity is safety; document it and hand it on.

## Quick notes for each person around the individual

### Family & relatives {.stage}
You are their memory, advocate and continuity. Share and update a one-page profile (interests, routines, sensory needs, early warning signs, what regulates them). Insist pain is excluded first for any new distress; protect routines, objects and interests; push for supported decision-making and a health passport; visit and connect through their interest.

### Doctors & care-home clinicians
Treat new or escalating distress as a **medical red flag** until pain, infection, constipation, dental problems and sensory/environmental causes are excluded. Screen for autistic burnout under chronic load. Use supported decision-making and a health passport; document effective accommodations.

### Support workers & PAs (residential staff)
You are the day-to-day environment. Consistent staff and sequence, visual schedules, preserved objects and interests, a calm meltdown/shutdown plan (reduce stimulation, ensure safety, no restraint). Document and share an individualised profile; advocate with family and clinicians.

### Nursing & residential-care staff
Pain and physical causes first; predictability and preserved routines; lower sensory load; supported decision-making; a calm, written meltdown/shutdown plan; a health passport for any admission. Read all behaviour as communication about unmet need.

## When to get more support {.support}
> Treat **new or escalating distress** as a medical red flag until pain, infection, constipation, dental problems and sensory/environmental causes are excluded. Screen for **autistic burnout** under chronic load. Involve **specialist autism / learning-disability teams** and speech-and-language (AAC). Plan every admission with a "hospital/health passport" and an agreed meltdown/shutdown plan that prioritises safety over restraint.

## Gender note {.gender}
> High-support women are especially under-identified and under-served; their distress is even more often misread. Apply the same rigour to excluding pain and overload across the whole team, regardless of presentation or communication method.
