“In a State of Flow”: A Qualitative Examination of Autistic Adults' Phenomenological Experiences of Task Immersion

Abstract

Background:

There is considerable conceptual overlap between Autistic hyperfocus and flow, yet these phenomena have seldom been associated in the literature. Moreover, no studies have provided an in-depth empirical investigation of Autistic people's flow-like or hyperfocus experiences. In this study, we sought to address this gap by investigating Autistic people's phenomenological experiences of task immersion, and how their experiences relate to existing conceptualizations of flow, hyperfocus, and monotropism.

Methods:

To address this aim, we drew on data from a broader qualitative study, designed in partnership with Autistic community members, to understand Autistic people's experiences of Autistic inertia. For that broader study, Autistic and non-Autistic researchers conducted semi-structured interviews with 24 Autistic adults about their everyday experiences of starting and stopping tasks. During these interviews, participants repeatedly discussed their experiences of task immersion and the benefits and challenges associated with it. We analyzed these responses specifically, using reflexive thematic analysis, adopting a combination of deductive and inductive approaches within an essentialist framework.

Results:

Our participants' accounts closely matched conceptual understandings of flow, Autistic hyperfocus, and monotropism. Specifically, the analysis revealed the all-encompassing nature of task immersion experiences, and how it could be difficult to “find the balance” between the joy and other aspects of everyday life. Despite the drawbacks, participants were glad to be immersed because they felt they could be themselves, with no pretences.

Conclusions:

These results are important for reframing task immersion as a state of great potential value to Autistic people's lives, but one that may require additional support if it is to play its role in enabling Autistic people to flourish.

Keywords: attention, monotropism, hyperfocus, well-being, quality of life

Community brief

Why is this an important issue?

Flow is a state of mind where a person becomes completely absorbed in what they are doing and does not notice anything around them. Autistic people's experiences of flow has not been studied enough, possibly because Autistic “voices” have not been taken seriously by autism researchers in the past. This means we know little about Autistic people's experiences of flow-like states.

What was the purpose of this study?

We wanted to know about Autistic people's experiences of flow and what effect these experiences had on their everyday lives.

What did the researchers do?

One Autistic and one non-autistic researcher spoke to 24 Autistic adults about their experiences of becoming fully engaged and focused on tasks. During the interview, we asked participants to tell us about moments when they felt so immersed in a task, or a thought, that they could not (or did not want to) stop. We also asked how not being able (or not wanting to) stop a task or a thought impacts their lives.

What were the results of the study?

Our Autistic participants' descriptions of their flow-like experiences seemed to be closely related to other concepts that are often talked about in the Autistic community, such as hyperfocus and monotropism. They spoke of how their flow-like experiences were all-encompassing and could bring much pleasure. But they also told us how flow could bring a great deal of struggle, as it could lead to exhaustion or neglect of other tasks (including personal care). Despite the challenges, participants enjoyed being in flow as it allowed them to be themselves.

What do these findings add to what was already known?

This study showed that Autistic flow (or “hyperfocus”) can be “the most amazing feeling in the world.” It also showed that being in flow can cause challenges. We need to work with Autistic community members to develop strategies to help Autistic people manage the negative effects on everyday life and physical health and well-being.

What are potential weaknesses in the study?

Most of our Autistic participants were women who were White, well-educated, and employed. Most also used traditional forms of communication. We do not know if our participants' flow-like experiences are similar to Autistic people who do not use traditional forms of communication, have an intellectual disability and come from different cultures. It will also be important to work with Autistic people and their allies to find new ways to investigate flow experiences in a diverse range of Autistic people.

How will these findings help autistic adults now or in the future?

These findings show how important it is to focus on things that can bring Autistic people joy. We need to know more about how increasing optimal flow experiences—at home, in schools, at work—could help Autistic people to thrive.

Background

“Where—in everyday life, in our normal experience—do we really feel happy?”1 The answer, Csikszentmihalyi suggests, is through entering “flow”—a psychological state whereby a person becomes so deeply immersed in a task that they pay no attention to usual demands and expectations.2,3 The flow state, as described by neurotypical people, appears to share considerable overlap with descriptions of Autistic “hyperfocus” whereby Autistic people become so absorbed in a task that they completely “tune out” everything else in their environment.4,5

Yet, despite the high correspondence between these conditions, the two phenomena have rarely been associated in the academic literature, although with a few exceptions.4–7 In this study, we sought to understand Autistic adults' phenomenological experiences of task immersion, and to examine whether these experiences amounted to flow-state experiences.

Csikszentmihalyi, founder of flow theory, originally identified six conditions that appear to be present when a person is experiencing flow (Fig. 1).3,8,9 First, while in flow, a person becomes so immersed in their activity that performance feels almost automatic, and they are aware of their actions but not of awareness itself. Second, flow experiences are characterized by the narrowing of attention on a limited set of stimuli, while “tuning out” potentially distracting information.

Third, there is a loss of ego or self-consciousness. Fourth, the person feels a sense of control over their actions and of the environment, without actively attempting to exert control. Fifth, the flow activity has coherent and non-contradictory demands for action and offers clear unambiguous immediate feedback in response to the person's actions. Sixth, flow experiences are autotelic (auto = self, telos = goal), meaning that the flow activity is intrinsically motivated and done for its own sake, with no motive of extrinsic reward.

More recently, Jackson and Marsh10 extended the list of flow conditions to nine, dividing Csikszentmihalyi's fifth condition (“coherent and non-contradictory demands”) into two—“clear goals” and “unambiguous feedback”—and adding “time transformation” (where time is perceived to pass differently than normal) and the “challenge-skill balance” (where a person's skills are just at the right level to cope with the high demands of the task).

In recent years, there has been a small but growing academic interest in how the flow state might be related to a similar phenomenon—namely, “Autistic hyperfocus.” Hyperfocus has been defined as a state of “complete absorption in a task, to a point where a person appears to completely ignore or ‘tune out’ everything else” (p. 1)4—which sounds remarkably similar to definitions of flow.

Indeed, in a conceptual article comparing the constructs of flow and hyperfocus, Ashinoff and Abu-Akel4 reviewed a small body of research examining flow states (largely derived from the positive psychology literature) and hyperfocus (derived from the cognitive psychology/psychiatry literature) in both neurotypical people and people diagnosed with conditions such as attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), autism, and schizophrenia.

They concluded that the two phenomena are descriptively similar or synonymous, and identified four common features: (1) an intense state of concentration, (2) a diminished perception of task-irrelevant stimuli, (3) task engagement for the sake of intrinsic reward (e.g., enjoyment or interest), and (4) an improvement in task performance, which comes with control over one's actions or thoughts (Fig. 1).

Despite the clear overlap between flow and hyperfocus, there are only a handful of articles that have examined either of these two phenomena,4–7,11 and, to our knowledge, no studies directly examining their phenomenology in Autistic people. This lack of research is consistent with a general trend to read Autistic experience negatively in terms of deficits, rather than associating it with positive, let alone joyful, states.5,7,12,13

Two recent studies investigated—among other things—Autistic adults' first-hand accounts of task immersion, including the advantages (e.g., joy, productivity, and the feeling of being in control) and disadvantages (e.g., lack of sleep and feeling compelled to complete a task) associated with hyperfocus.7,11 In both articles, however, discussion of the connection between Autistic hyperfocus and flow states was fleeting.

One possible reason for the lack of research on Autistic flow and hyperfocus is that Autistic testimony has not always been taken seriously by academics, but instead viewed as offering little more than anecdotal evidence.14,15 This oversight has extended to the Autistic-driven theory of Autistic cognition, namely monotropism16–18—the notion that Autistic people tend to focus intensely on, and concentrate resources in, one or a few interests or tasks at any time (Fig. 1), compared with non-autistic people, who are thought to have a polytropic tendency, or multiple interests aroused at any time.

The monotropic state or “hyper-awareness within the attention tunnel,”17(p. 142) leaves fewer resources for other processes, and is thought to be a gateway to achieving intense experiences and flow.18 Disregard for the theory of monotropism,* and autistic testimony in general, has likely hampered progress in examining the association between autism and flow experiences.

The current study

In sum, we currently know exceedingly little about Autistic people's phenomenological experiences of task immersion. We sought to address this gap by drawing on data from a broader qualitative study designed to understand Autistic people's experiences of Autistic inertia.19 For that study, we interviewed Autistic adults about their everyday experiences of starting and stopping tasks and the impact of these states on their everyday lives.

During these interviews, our participants repeatedly discussed their experiences of task immersion and the benefits and challenges associated with it. In this study, we analyzed these responses specifically to (1) examine Autistic people's first-person accounts of flow-like states; (2) understand how Autistic people's experiences map onto existing conceptualizations of flow,8,10 hyperfocus4 and monotropism17; and (3) generate hypotheses for future research into Autistic task immersion.

Methods

Participants

We advertised the study through social media with a video and flyer. Eligible participants had to be (1) fluent in English, (2) living in Australia, (3) aged 18 years or older, and (4) clinically diagnosed or self-identified as Autistic (the latter to account for the often-significant barriers in gaining a clinical diagnosis).20 Of the 53 expressions of interest, we excluded 29 as they did not live in Australia (n = 9), complete the consent form (n = 9), confirm an interview appointment (n = 5), return email interview responses (n = 4), or attend the interview (n = 2).

The remaining 24 participants were aged 19–71 years (M = 44.3; SD = 16.4). Eleven (46%) identified as women, seven (29%) as men, four (17%) as non-binary/genderqueer, and two (8%) as “other.” Participants reported themselves to be predominantly White, highly educated, and currently employed (Table 1). All had an independent clinical diagnosis of autism (M age of diagnosis = 37.3 years, SD = 17.3; range: 3–61 years) from a clinical psychologist (n = 17; 71%), a team of health professionals (n = 4; 17%), a psychiatrist (n = 2; 8%), or an autism specialist (n = 1; 4%). Most reported co-occurring diagnoses, especially depression, anxiety disorder, and ADHD (Table 1).

Procedure

Macquarie University Human Research Ethics Committee granted ethical approval for this study (Project ID: 520211064335595). All participants provided written informed consent before taking part.

To begin, participants completed a brief online questionnaire, powered by LimeSurvey (Version 5.3.32; LimeSurvey GmbH, Hamburg, Germany), providing demographic and diagnostic information. Next, we organized individual semi-structured interviews with participants through their preferred means of communication. H.R. and H.C. conducted the interviews between January and March 2022.

Before the interview, we sent participants the primary interview questions to accommodate processing differences and to allow them to prepare their responses if they wished. We asked open-ended questions about their everyday experiences of starting and stopping tasks, including how they felt during these states, the frequency and duration of these states, their perceived causes, things that help them to move out of these two states, and the impact of these states on their everyday lives (see Supplementary Data Semi-Structured Interview Questions).

Although we avoided questions asking directly about experiences of “flow” or “hyperfocus,” we, nevertheless, elicited participants' experiences of moments when they “felt so immersed in a task, or a thought, that they couldn't (or didn't want to) stop.” We focus on these responses specifically in the analysis reported hereunder.

We conducted the interviews predominantly over Zoom (n = 18; 75%; email: n = 5, 21%; phone: n = 1; 4%). Spoken and written interviews varied in length between 34 and 107 minutes (M = 61 minutes; SD = 19.7), and 637 and 3800 words (M = 1889; SD = 1262), respectively. Participants received an AUD 25 voucher for their time. We recorded all spoken interviews for later transcription with participants' prior permission, which were subsequently transcribed verbatim. We sent participants their interview transcript so that they could review it and remove or amend any details before analysis.

Data analysis

We followed Braun and Clarke's21 method for reflexive thematic analysis. Our epistemological stance fits within an essentialist framework, in which we report the experiences, meanings, and reality of the participants. Our qualitative analysis was informed by our collective experience and training in psychology (H.R., H.C., W.L., and E.P.) education (K.P.-P. and E.P.), computer science (K.P.-P.) and creativity and mental health (J.A.), as well as positionalities as Autistic people (J.A., H.C., and W.L.).

We used a combination of deductive (top-down) and inductive (bottom-up) approaches22 to understand our Autistic participants' phenomenological experiences of flow and determine how our participants' experiences mapped (or failed to map) onto existing conceptual understandings of flow, hyperfocus, and/or monotropism. Critically, we integrated these approaches at the coding (rather than theme-generating) stage to ensure that our participants' experiences and perspectives were privileged in our analysis.

To begin, H.R. selected all sections of participants' transcripts that referred to experiences of flow. H.R. and E.P. read and re-read these excerpts, taking reflexive notes, which included striking and recurring observations, “real-time” thoughts and feelings (which might have related to researcher assumptions), speculations, potential underpinning meanings, and ongoing queries and questions for the team.

They then deductively applied codes following the nine dimensions of flow,2,3,10 as well as key aspects of monotropism,17 the theory on which one of us (W.L.) was a primary contributor (Fig. 1). In the subsequent inductive (data-driven) stage, H.R. and E.P. re-examined the transcripts to detect aspects of interviewees' experiences that might not be accommodated within the deductive analysis and generated additional codes (including, e.g., the broader context of flow-like experiences and the impact of such experiences).

Next, E.P. examined the deductively and inductively generated codes to identify similarities and differences, and merged them, where appropriate. We then clustered codes together to identify potential themes and subthemes, focusing on semantic features of the data (staying close to the interviewees' language rather than theoretically driven terms). E.P. produced a draft thematic map, and the relevant data were collated under each theme and subtheme. The broader team discussed and reviewed the draft analysis multiple times, during which the themes and subthemes were reflexively revised and refined.21

Community involvement

E.P. secured the funding (on which W.L. was a named collaborator) and brought together a team of Autistic (J.A., H.C., and W.L.) and non-autistic (H.R., K.P.-P., and E.P.) researchers, who worked together on the broader project on Autistic inertia.19 J.A., H.C., and W.L. were involved from its inception, meeting regularly over Zoom to discuss in-depth the design of the study, the ethical issues raised and the methods. H.C. was employed to interview half of our Autistic participants.

Once data collection was complete, the team met regularly to discuss and interpret the findings—including the current analysis focused on task immersion. An Autistic undergraduate intern also contributed to these discussions. Overall, our Autistic partners' input resulted in collaborative decisions that improved the relevance and accessibility of the materials; the nature and content of the interviews; the precision of the resulting themes and subthemes; and the conceptual clarity of the write-up of this article.

Results

We identified three themes in the data (Fig. 2). We use illustrative quotes throughout (attributed through participant pseudonymized initials).

Theme 1: “It's just total absorption”

Throughout the interviews, our participants used a variety of terms, including being “hyper-focused,” “immersed,” “in a state of flow,” “in the zone,” or “on a roll” to depict their “total absorption” (DA) in tasks, ranging from creative activities to more mundane ones. These experiences were reported to be “all-encompassing” (KN) and “so intense that I lose touch with everything else” (subtheme 1.1): “I hyper-focused… so, I'm focused on the completion of the task to the exclusion of the environment” (DA). Being “completely immersed in that particular thing” also meant that participants would “lose track of anything else outside of that” (GC), as one participant explained:

Particularly if I'm studying or learning about something new, or preparing a talk for a conference, or something that really taps into my passions. Once I get started, stopping is not an option, it's like riding a bike down a very steep hill, you just keep going. Not eating, not drinking, trying to avoid going to the toilet, overriding everything. (MC)

Another participant recounted how they have alarms set on their phone to remind them to take their medication “but if I'm making a piece of art, I won't hear that go off, even though it's just repeating… it's almost like walls go up between me and what I'm doing and everything else” (VA).

Some described being consciously aware of bodily sensations (“it's like a stubborn sense of enjoying the space that my mind and body is in and not wanting to interrupt that with something as mundane as sleep”; BD), whereas others were emphatic about having “absolutely no recognition of my body's signals—I won't feel hungry, I won't feel thirsty, I won't need to go to the toilet. Or I probably do but I don't notice it” (KN). Some went so far as to say they were “not sure I'm even aware I have a body at such times” (CK), which could mean going “for days without sleep” (JR).

Participants' shift of awareness extended beyond bodily sensations, however. They also reported experiencing an altered perception of time (subtheme 1.2)—“losing all sense of time” (ET), “not being aware of time passing” (MC), “feeling like time runs into each other” (KT) and feeling “almost like time stops” (NS), whether it be reading, working or “going down a rabbit hole of research” (GC). Others reported being “not really conscious of time passing or anything happening around me” (ET); “I'm only focused on the task and that's all that exists” (VA). Ultimately, being “in that moment” (CH) in this way could mean “I will be doing whatever I'm doing, and it feels like half an hour—but it's been four hours, six hours, an entire day” (KN).

Participants reported experiencing “laser focus” attention (MS) (subtheme 1.3) or “tunnel vision” (BB)—a “real intense ability to switch off from everything else and just focus on that one thing” (NS) to the extent that “everything else disappears” (SM). This focus was seen as a strength and felt to be the product of “how our brains are wired” (NS). Such intensity of focus also reportedly gave them a sense of clarity—“a clear state of mind” (BD), a moment in which “my brain is clearly focused on the task” (SM). In the absence of distraction, participants felt that they could dedicate “every resource I have” (MC) to the task at hand and “make sure that your whole brain is just functioning the best that it can” (CH).

Participants also shared information about the content of their absorption, which could vary substantially (subtheme 1.4). They spoke of how these hyperfocus experiences could happen “if I'm reading about a subject or watching a movie or a documentary that I'm very interested in” (CF), or if I'm studying or learning about something new (MC)—especially “something that I'm really passionate about” (VA).

Others recalled being in flow-like states while doing domestic chores, writing, dancing, reading, organizing the home, making videos, researching autism or gardening. Indeed, some went so far as to say, “when I get into that mindset, it doesn't matter how menial the task. I absolutely love what I'm doing” (MC). People also described being “drawn into” sensory aspects of the environment:

I just would get taken up by shapes, things changing. It was the same watching water. I was lost a couple of times as a 3-year-old, I found my way to the beach, got out of the garden, took myself off to the seaside. And was found on a sandbank, cut off, with the tide coming in and stranded. I remember, the girl who lived next door, she yelled at me not to move, and I remember thinking, “why would I want to move? I'm fine here.” Just totally mystified by the light reflecting in the water. (LB)

Others acknowledged that “it probably happens to different extents in different things” (KN), distinguishing between “things where I get into that really intense, “losing all sense of time” state—generally creative things”—from “when I'm doing things like playing those stupid games on my phone, like Candy Crush … it's definitely not the same all-encompassing, hyper-focus experience” (KN). Similarly, others insisted that these states happened mostly “within the intellectually stimulating” (MK) and “if I'm doing something creative, and it's something that I enjoy… where the ideas are just bouncing off each other and there's things just coming left, right and centre” (CH). One participant explained:

It doesn't happen if I'm watching a movie or TV … I can feel connected and immersed if I'm watching Spider-Man or something in the movies, but I don't feel any difficulty in extracting myself. But when it's an intellectual thing, even having an intellectual conversation to a certain extent, it's like I walk in a different world, somehow. (MK)

Theme 2: “Finding the balance” can be “a serious struggle”

Despite a lack of consistency regarding the focus of these flow-like experiences, participants were, nevertheless, unanimous in their descriptions of how exhilarating these moments could be. As one participant put it, it is “the most amazing feeling in the world” (CH). Nevertheless, they also reported how that “nice feeling of being engrossed in something” (CK) often made it “very hard to let go” (subtheme 2.1). Some interviewees spoke of how “there's no thought of, ‘I need to stop’” (DA).

Others, however, reporting not wanting, or not being able, to stop because they had “a physical desire and mental desire to continue doing what I'm doing” (BD), were “completely zoned into what I'm doing” (CK), and/or “had to see something through to completion” (VA). Consequently, they often spoke about “not wanting to be disturbed” (BD) because “that would generally be it… that would end it” (MS).

Being “startled” (JR) or interrupted in this way could be “such a shock” (MS)—but the negative effects of having to “transition away” (BD) from that focus of attention could be even greater (“so mentally draining for me”; SM), leaving them “confused” (VA) and making it “very hard to get motivated again” (DA), especially if they “haven't finished a task” (NS).

Participants also described that the intensity of focus can be “exhausting” (subtheme 2.2): “if it's something I'm really enjoying, and I don't want to stop because I'm in a sense of flow, it feels really good, but then I am wiped at the end of the day” (GC). Others agreed that when “I'm focused on that end goal to the exclusion of all else and then, all of a sudden, I have to take my attention away, work for a little while to motivate myself to do whatever the alternative is… I focus on that, now I have to come back and start all over again. And I'm left exhausted” (DA).

Participants reported feeling “tired, often cranky, completely zonked” immediately after a period of being “engrossed in something” (CK) and the physical impact of when the “focus eventually breaks and I then realise that I've been sitting in the same position for six hours and I'm starving, and I desperately need to pee and every muscle in my body hurts” (KN). One participant recounted the cumulative effects of the challenges managing periods of absorption with “my other chores” and “relaxation time that I need to decompress to be able to face the next day”: “you start wearing yourself out, and then you start heading towards meltdowns and things like that, because you start burning yourself out” (CH).

Participants also spoke of how moments of “really intense hyper-focus” (KN) could have significant repercussions on other aspects of people's lives (subtheme 2.3). They spoke of how “it definitely affects the balance… [which] is all off a lot of the time; I'm not taking care of myself because I'm doing too much good stuff” (MS). They further described how it could affect “being able to go to sleep or get ready for the next day” (DJ), “being late for work” (EB) or “eating enough and having my medication regularly and that kind of thing. Because those things can slip to the side a bit” (VA).

People described being “oblivious to some of the chaos that I may have caused” (DA) and finding it challenging to attend to essential household chores (“I might keep going through to, I don't know, seven, eight o'clock at night… whereas I haven't done the shopping, I haven't done the laundry”; CH) or work-related duties: “it can be great to get totally engrossed in something… but completely impractical. And suddenly I've missed several writing deadlines I was hoping to meet, and I've fallen behind in my university reading, and things are generally looking pretty bleak” (CK).

Some also spoke of how they could impact their parenting duties: “Just focusing on that one thing… until I smell something burning in the kitchen because my kids have said, ‘mum's not doing dinner, so I'm going to do something myself’” (NS). One participant described how far-reaching these challenges could be:

I was so lost in it, and I love it so much that I was sitting in my head just absorbing all this fantastic information and creating and all that. But I found it really hard to parent during that time, to be a social being, to be in love, to have relationships, or whatever because I was so absorbed by that. (MK)

It is, therefore, not surprising that our participants also reported feeling guilty about “engaging in things that I can't break away from” (subtheme 2.4) because it could cause their family, friends and colleagues “a lot of concern” (BB). They described feeling “like a burden to everyone” (MJ), who they often relied upon to “pull me out of it” (SM): “I tend to be very good at focusing on the tasks I'm involved in, and when that focus is combined with a certain level of interest, then it becomes almost impossible to stop without an intervention” (CK).

They also felt that their “exclusive focus” (DA) was often under-recognized by others around them as a strength, an “ability to look at the detail in things” (NS) and, as such, could also be undermined. One participant described how “when I was little and to this day, my mum said it was insane how I could sit there and watch the same Disney movie over and over and over and not move” (SM).

Another spoke of how “that intensity about doing something made it sometimes difficult for people to accommodate me… I was so determined to make good art and I did and got the recognition for it. But I remember my ex-husband saying something like, ‘why are you so obsessive about it? It's good to do art but you don't have to kill yourself’” (DM).

Theme 3: “I get to be me”

Despite “the downsides of it” (KN), our participants were adamant that these flow-like or hyperfocus states brought them “a lot of joy” (VA) and made them feel “energised” (BB)—to the extent that it “almost feels like it's beyond enjoyment… this is all that exists.” They described that this joy was in part because they were in a space in which they could “get to be me” (CF)—with “no pretences” (subtheme 3.1):

I don't get to be me very often. I often try and temper the rest, and I hate it. Sometimes it really gets to me, because neurotypicals have this thing called manners, and being polite. There's certain etiquettes and things like that. When you really just want to say what you think… I spend my life taming that, so I don't get into a bad situation. So, when you finally get yourself in a situation where you can be yourself, and be as honest as possible with no pretences, it's a nice feeling. (CF)

Others agreed that these experiences were a relief from “just feeling exhausted or pressure from the world” (MJ), thus allowing “you to be more yourself” (CK). One participant described this experience in-depth:

It's when that internal voice stops. No, stop's not the right word. You're not second-guessing, you're not worrying about what anybody else is thinking. You're not worried about anything. You're just existing. It's purely being who you are, kind of thing. And they're just pure thoughts. (CH)

Many, perhaps as a result, also reported how “hyper-focusing in mind” (DA) made them “feel good about myself” (subtheme 3.2). For example, one participant reported getting lost “in a really good book where I'm living that life of characters—I feel happy, excited. My brain is being stimulated… It can improve my mood. It gets the endorphins flowing and I can feel better to some degree” (RK).

Participants spoke of how their focus on the task at hand was often motivated by a “drive to achieve that endpoint” (DA) and a “sense of completion” (VA). They also described how satisfying it can be to “know you're achieving things or learning things” (BD) “and producing stuff that is really good” (KN). As one participant put it:

The flow feels really good while I'm in it. It's fantastic. I'm actually doing something. I'm actually making progress, or I'm actually helping. I'm actually making a difference… It's like I'm having a really good time for once. I'm doing something that I like to do. (MS)

Inherent to these reports was a strong sense of control (subtheme 3.3)—or as one participant described it, a “relief to be in a regulated state—and I want to stay that way” (BD). One participant gave an example:

When I'm writing a report, my body is regulated through the calm nature of typing and referring to my materials while my mind is in a state of flow, therefore I don't want to be disturbed in either way. I feel a physical desire and a mental desire to continue doing what I'm doing.

People spoke of it being “good for my mental health… doing something that is comforting, or soothing or calming” (ET), and allowed them to “be more relaxed” (DA), at least in the moment. They also reported the “pleasure or satisfaction [from] having something to do that we want to do” (NS) and the sense of agency it afforded: “I know what I'm doing. I know what's happening around me. I can predict what's going to occur and the fact that things occur as I can predict them, in a stable organised way, gives me a sense of satisfaction” (DA).

Discussion

In this study, we report an in-depth qualitative investigation of Autistic people's phenomenological experiences of task immersion. While our analysis drew on existing conceptual understandings of flow, hyperfocus, and monotropism, we also sought to identify aspects of interviewees' experiences that may not have been accommodated within these understandings.

Our participants' responses were related to the nine conditions of flow state experiences.3,8–10 First, and consistent with the flow condition of “action-awareness merging,” our participants described becoming so immersed in tasks that they would lose awareness of their biological signals and of the broader sensory environment. Second, they reported “laser-focused attention” and the ability to tune out information that was not task-relevant, which corresponds to a second condition of flow—the centering of attention on a limited stimulus field.

Third, when deeply immersed in tasks, our participants were unconcerned with what others thought about them, which relates to Csikszentmihalyi's8 “loss of self-consciousness” flow condition. Fourth, task immersion was accompanied by participants” sense of mastery over their actions and environment (“I know what I'm doing. I know what is happening around me…”), which corresponds to the “sense of control” condition of flow.

Fifth, and consistent with the “clear goals” condition, our participants described having a clear state of mind and confidence that they knew what to do to achieve the “end goal.” Sixth, while performing a task, participants described receiving feedback in such a way that they could “predict what's going to occur” (subtheme 3.3), in line with the flow condition of “unambiguous feedback.”

Seventh, our participants felt pleasure and satisfaction when doing tasks that they had an intrinsic drive to achieve, which relates to the autotelic nature of flow experience. Eighth, our participants experienced an altered perception of time, not being aware of time passing and feeling like time stops while immersed, in line with the “time transformation” condition.

And finally, consistent with Jackson and Marsh's10 “challenge-skill balance” condition, some participants described experiencing intense task immersion only for tasks that were intellectually challenging or creative, such as reading, studying, learning, or having an intellectual conversation. It should be noted that not all nine conditions are necessary to enter flow—for example, some people may experience flow even in situations where the challenge is not perfectly balanced with their skill level. Overall, as our participants' accounts of task immersion collectively mapped fairly well onto all nine conditions of flow, it seems reasonable to describe these accounts in terms of flow state experiences.

Our participants' accounts of task immersion are also consistent with the four descriptive features common to flow and Autistic hyperfocus, outlined by Ashinoff and Abu-Akel.4 Specifically, our participants reported (1) an intense state of focus, (2) a diminished awareness of task-irrelevant inputs, (3) intrinsic motivation to carry out a task, and (4) a sense of mastery and control over their task performance.

Their reports also correspond to descriptions of the monotropic state,18 which is thought to be a precursor to achieving intense experiences and flow. Consistent with monotropism, participants repeatedly recounted experiencing intense “tunnel vision,” where their attention is captured on a small number of interests at any time to the extent that they “lose touch with everything else.” In sum, our participants' accounts generally map onto conceptual understandings of flow, Autistic hyperfocus, and monotropism.

Autistic flow-like experiences may have been largely overlooked by autism researchers in the past because autism has historically been understood in terms of deficits7,12,13 rather than assets, such as a propensity for flow experiences.5 Indeed, while positive psychology posits that “a good life is one that is characterized by complete absorption in what one does,”3 Autistic task immersion has been described as “abnormal in intensity or focus,”23 as well as maladaptive as it can interfere with daily functioning and limit opportunities for social interaction.24

Unfortunately, when a characteristic—such as task immersion—is framed as a deficit, it is likely to be discouraged even if beneficial. The existence of autism intervention programs designed to reduce the occurrence of interests that are “unusual in their narrowness of focus, duration of time and/or intensity”25 (p. 1242) is a case in point. If it is indeed the case that Autistic hyperfocus and flow are one and the same, then discouraging hyperfocus may deny Autistic people flow state experiences.7

This study urges researchers to think anew about Autistic task immersion, as something that can also be “the most amazing feeling in the world” and, in many instances, should be celebrated. Rather than automatically discouraging task immersion, autism researchers should be working toward identifying activities that Autistic people find intrinsically motivating, and in doing so, pinpointing areas where flow experiences can be increased when desirable.26

Considering the high rates of depressive disorders in the Autistic community27 (in our study alone, 75% of the participants also reported a diagnosis of depression), increasing flow experiences could be particularly important for improving mental health outcomes and fostering Autistic flourishing,28 especially if they coincide with improvements in Autistic people's sense of control over their own lives and their ability to pursue their interests in an authentic way, independent of the stereotypical judgments of others.

Indeed, one study29 has demonstrated that enabling autistic students to access their interests and passions in school had positive effects both on the students' learning and well-being, and on teachers' pedagogical practices, which became more relational and empathetic in nature (see also Koegel et al.30).

Nevertheless, it is also clear from our results that “finding a balance” between these immersive experiences and other aspects of everyday life is also critical. Our participants reported how the intense absorption in single activities often affects their self-care, work, and family life. They spoke of the practical challenges that flow imposed, as other responsibilities, duties, and tasks were sometimes overlooked as a result of the immersive influence of flow.

They also emphasized the detrimental effects that such intense engagement can have on their physical health and well-being, especially with regard to fatigue, sleep, eating patterns, and general self-care. These sentiments echo the findings from a recent study on Autistic young people's engagement in online video gaming,31 which also highlighted the challenges young people had balancing their immersion in gaming with other responsibilities, such as self-care and homework. Future research should examine how best to support Autistic people to balance engagement in flow experiences with self-care.

The capacity to experience flow seems to be almost universal.3 But the challenges posed by Autistic people's experiences of flow as reported in this study, also lead to the question of whether there is something distinctive about Autistic people's flow experiences. For example, it is possible that flow experiences are more intense, longer, or frequently experienced by Autistic people (consistent with a monotropic cognitive style18), and that the subject of focus is more wide-ranging, including both challenging tasks and those that are more mundane (e.g., household chores).

Such experiences might also be more difficult from which to transition.19 Being interrupted while in flow may be experienced as more distressing for Autistic than non-autistic people, also making it harder for Autistic people to re-enter flow states after an interruption. More research is needed to investigate these possibilities, including through administering standardized flow scales32 to Autistic and non-autistic participants to compare their experiences. The findings from this study and those from future work should also inform the development of a flow scale developed by and for Autistic people.

Another open question is whether Autistic flow and ADHD hyperfocus4 might be related phenomena—and whether they both might be underpinned by monotropism. This question is of particular interest as half of our participants reported a co-occurring diagnosis of ADHD, and anecdotal references to hyperfocus are abundant in the ADHD community.33

Although research into ADHD hyperfocus is, at present, extremely limited,4,34,35 one recent study presented intriguing data showing that being Autistic and/or having ADHD were significantly associated with higher scores on a newly devised monotropism questionnaire.36 More work is needed to determine precisely how experiences of task immersion are shared across neurodivergent profiles, and whether the same underlying mechanism(s) is involved.

Limitations

Our study was limited in several ways. First, our methods and recruitment strategy may have resulted in a relatively homogenous sample. Our participants were predominantly White highly educated employed women, and most communicated using spoken language. Furthermore, the highly verbal nature of the interview may have discouraged or excluded Autistic adults who do not use traditional forms of communication and/or have an intellectual disability.

Future studies should use maximum variation sampling,37 purposively recruiting Autistic participants with diverse backgrounds and experiences. It will also be important to work in partnership with Autistic people and their allies to design and implement innovative methods to understand the flow experiences of Autistic people who also have an intellectual disability and/or are non- or minimally speaking.

Second, despite such homogeneity, there was wide variability with regard to one background characteristic—the interviewees' age (which ranged from 19 to 71 years). Although recent research suggests a weak-to-negligible relationship between age and flow experiences in the neurotypical population,38 it is entirely possible that flow experiences differ at different stages of Autistic people's lives. We could not examine any such developmental differences due to the qualitative nature of our study, but this issue could be examined in future research.

Finally, this analysis was drawn from a broader study designed to understand Autistic inertia. Although understanding task immersion was integral to that study and our interview questions yielded rich responses, it is possible that a study focused directly on flow states may have revealed more.

Conclusions

The Autistic participants in our study presented their experiences of task immersion and flow as a mixed blessing, bringing joy and a sense of control to lives often filled with challenge, but also presenting a number of important difficulties of their own. Such results should be important for reframing task immersion or “Autistic flow” as a state of great potential value to Autistic people's lives. More research is needed to understand the role that Autistic flow can play in enabling Autistic people to flourish.28
